Project Overview

What this project was about

IGNYTE AI is an internal recruiting intelligence platform built for Kingsley Gate, an executive search firm. The goal was simple: help recruiters find and evaluate the best candidates faster — without sacrificing the quality of their judgment.

Project
IGNYTE AI — Recruiting Intelligence Platform

Lead UX Designer — end to end, solo

Goal
Design an AI recruiting platform that helps recruiters identify the best candidates in minutes, not hours
The Problem

Recruiters were drowning in manual work

Before IGNYTE AI, finding and evaluating candidates at Kingsley Gate was slow, fragmented, and exhausting. Recruiters had to rely on gut instinct and multiple disconnected tools — with no smart system to help them prioritise.

⏱ 3–5 hours to review candidates for a single role
Recruiters needed to identify the best candidates in minutes — not days of manual review.

The existing process required opening multiple tools, reading hundreds of resumes from scratch, and making evaluation decisions with very little supporting data. There was no way to quickly know which candidates were worth deeper attention — and which could be safely deprioritised.

Manual resume scanning — recruiters read hundreds of resumes one by one, with no prioritisation signal to guide where to start
Fragmented tools — candidate evaluation required switching between email, spreadsheets, LinkedIn, and an ATS that didn't talk to each other
AI with no explanation — existing AI recommendations just showed a score. Recruiters didn't trust what they couldn't understand
No side-by-side comparison — comparing two candidates meant jumping between separate screens, making it hard to make confident decisions

Research

Listening before designing

Understanding the real problem required spending time with the people actually doing the work — not just looking at the existing product. I ran four research methods across several weeks.

8 Recruiter interviews
12 hours of workflow observation
3 live candidate search reviews
Competitive audit — 6 ATS & sourcing tools
We expected to find that recruiters needed better search filters. We were wrong. The core problem wasn't discovery — it was evaluation. Recruiters could find candidates; they couldn't confidently assess them at executive level. That single realisation reframed the entire product.

Interviews revealed that the three things recruiters cared most about were: speed (get to good candidates faster), accuracy (trust the right signal, not just keyword matches), and trust in AI (only accept recommendations they could understand and verify).

Key Insights

What research revealed

Five clear insights emerged from the research that directly shaped the design decisions I made.

Insight 01
Ranked lists beat complex filters
Recruiters don't want to configure 15 filters. They want to open the platform and immediately see their strongest candidates at the top — sorted by relevance, not alphabetical order.
Insight 02
AI only earns trust when it explains itself
When AI showed only a score, recruiters ignored it or actively pushed back. When it showed the signals behind the score — career trajectory, scope of past roles, growth rate — they immediately trusted it more.
Insight 03
Comparison needs to happen in one place
Recruiters were jumping between tabs to compare candidates. Every context switch broke their train of thought. They needed to compare candidates side by side without leaving the platform.
Insight 04
Signal clarity matters more than raw data
Showing more data didn't help — it overwhelmed. What recruiters actually needed was clarity: what is this candidate's strength, and why does it matter for this specific role?
Insight 05
Hiring managers were the hidden bottleneck
Hiring manager feedback was coming in via email and Slack — days late, unstructured, and hard to act on. Bringing that feedback loop inside the product was key to reducing shortlist rebuild time.
Design Principles

From insights to principles

Each insight translated into a design principle that guided every decision — from information hierarchy to when to show or hide complexity.

🔍
AI transparency
Never show a score without an explanation. Every AI recommendation must show the signals behind it — so recruiters can verify the reasoning and decide whether they agree. Applied on every screen that shows AI output.
📐
Progressive disclosure
Show the most important signal first — the AI match score. Make deeper details (competency breakdown, career signals, full profile) available on demand. Recruiters shouldn't have to work to get an overview.
🧠
Reduce cognitive load
Managing 300 candidates across 15 open roles is too complex to hold in one person's head. The design must do the prioritisation work — through ranked lists, clear status, and smart defaults — so recruiters can focus on decisions, not organisation.
🤝
Contextual workflows
Recruiters shouldn't have to leave the platform to evaluate, share, or get feedback on candidates. Discovery, evaluation, and collaboration all happen in one connected workspace — no more emailing PDFs or messaging over Slack.
Design Decisions

Why I made each key design choice

The most important decisions weren't about visual style — they were about how to structure information and when to let AI lead versus when to let the recruiter lead.

Decision 01
AI-ranked lists instead of filter systems
Problem
Recruiters faced hundreds of unranked candidates with complex filter interfaces. Setting filters took time, required expertise, and still didn't surface the best candidates reliably.
Design Decision
Replace complex filter-first interfaces with an AI-ranked default. Candidates appear sorted by match score from the moment the recruiter opens a search. Filters are still available — but secondary, not the starting point.
Result
Recruiters immediately see their strongest candidates first. The time to reach a credible shortlist dropped significantly because they stopped wasting time on low-fit profiles at the bottom of a long list.
Decision 02
Score with visible signals, not just a number
Problem
Early prototypes showed a single aggregate match score per candidate. Recruiters rejected it immediately — they couldn't interrogate it, so they didn't trust it.
Design Decision
Show the signals that generate the score — leadership trajectory, career scope growth, decision-making style — alongside the overall number. Evidence comes first, score comes last.
Result
Once recruiters could see and verify the reasoning behind a score, adoption resistance dropped completely. Transparency was the feature — not the score itself.
Decision 03
One unified candidate profile instead of scattered tools
Problem
Candidate information lived across LinkedIn, the ATS, a spreadsheet, and email. Evaluating a single candidate required opening 4+ tabs and mentally combining everything.
Design Decision
Build a single profile view that combines resume data, AI competency signals, career insights, and hiring manager feedback — all in one screen, with a consistent structure.
Result
Recruiters could fully evaluate a candidate and move them to the next pipeline stage without leaving the profile page. Evaluation time per candidate dropped significantly.

Reflection

What I learned from this project

Looking back, three lessons stand out — about AI, about trust, and about who you're actually designing for.

What worked well
AI transparency was the real product
The biggest design challenge wasn't the AI itself — it was trust. Recruiters didn't distrust scores because they were wrong. They distrusted them because they couldn't see why. Once we showed the underlying signals, adoption shifted completely. Transparency wasn't a feature we added on top — it was the core of the product.
What we got wrong early
Too many signals overwhelmed instead of helped
Early designs displayed every available AI signal at once. Recruiters felt overwhelmed and stopped trusting the platform. The fix was progressive disclosure — show the top-level match score first, and reveal the detailed signal breakdown only when the recruiter actively wants to go deeper. Less visible at once meant more understood.
What I would improve next
Better explainability and bias auditing
We designed responsible safeguards into the UI — manual override, signal transparency, human confirmation before any candidate advances. But the scoring model's training data was never formally audited for demographic bias during my involvement. If I were starting again, I'd push for that audit from day one. Responsible AI design has to go deeper than the interface.
The hidden insight
Hiring managers were the bottleneck we almost missed
Hiring managers weren't the primary users — but they were the decision-makers. Designing the collaboration workspace specifically around their review behaviour (not the recruiter's workflow) was the single change that most improved shortlist alignment speed. If you're designing a B2B tool, always ask: who else in the organisation needs to act for this product to succeed?
