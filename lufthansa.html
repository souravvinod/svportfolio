03 Insights

What the Research Revealed

Recurring patterns from interviews and field observations — the signals that shaped the design direction.

Research Insights
01
Bottleneck
Manual Processing Creates Operational Bottlenecks
Evidence
  • Drivers waited 15–30 min during peak hours
  • Staff manually verify every booking reference
  • Only one driver processed at a time
Insight

The system limits processing capacity because verification is entirely manual — creating a single-lane bottleneck at peak times.

"

Sometimes I wait half an hour just to show my documents.

— Cargo Truck Driver
02
Visibility
Drivers Lack Process Visibility
Evidence
  • Drivers asked "Where is my dock?" repeatedly
  • Drivers returned to counter to clarify next steps
Insight

Drivers lack clear guidance about what happens after check-in — creating unnecessary back-and-forth with staff.

03
Language
Language Barriers Slow Communication & Increase Errors
Evidence
  • 65% of observed drivers were non-native English speakers
  • 40% from Eastern Europe (Polish, Romanian, Czech)
  • 25% from South Asia (Turkish, Urdu, Hindi)
  • Staff used gestures or repeated phrases in 6/12 sessions
  • English-only forms caused 3 of the 7 AWB entry errors
Insight

Language differences directly cause documentation errors and slow the check-in process — a multilingual kiosk with icon-led flows removes this friction entirely.

"

I don't understand the form — my company gives me papers, I just show them.

— Driver (Polish), field observation
04
Data Entry
Manual Data Entry Is the Primary Source of Documentation Errors
Evidence — 20 observed sessions
  • 7/20 check-ins had incorrect AWB numbers entered
  • 4/20 had missing or incomplete paperwork
  • 3/20 had wrong dock assignments due to staff error
  • Total error rate across observed sessions: 70%
  • Each error added 4–8 min of resolution time
Insight

Manual data entry under time pressure is the dominant source of errors. Structured digital input with real-time validation would eliminate the majority of these errors at the point of entry.

"

AWB numbers are long — drivers type them wrong all the time. We have to look it up manually to find their booking.

— Terminal staff interview
05
Environment
Operational Environment Impacts Usability
Evidence
  • Drivers wear protective work gloves
  • Interaction after long driving hours
  • Noisy, time-pressured terminal environment
Insight

Interfaces must support large touch targets and minimal typing to be reliable in real terminal conditions.

Why not mobile?

Field research found that 3 of 8 interviewed drivers used company-issued phones with restricted app installs. Terminal areas also have inconsistent connectivity. A kiosk provides a universal, always-on access point that works for every driver regardless of device access or digital literacy.

While scheduling contributes to peak congestion, the research specifically isolated the check-in interaction at the gate as the consistently controllable bottleneck — the one step where UX intervention delivers the most measurable impact.

Ten screens covering the full driver journey — from idle welcome state to check-in completion. Each screen decision is grounded in the research insights, design principles and information architecture defined in the Opportunities phase.

Design Approach
Dark industrial UI system — built for terminal environments
The interface uses a midnight blue palette (--bg: #06091A) with high-contrast yellow accents (#FFD000) and white text. This colour scheme was chosen specifically for cargo terminal environments: dark backgrounds reduce glare from harsh overhead lighting, while yellow accents provide 9.8:1 contrast ratio — exceeding WCAG AA by 2×. Every typographic decision, spacing unit and interactive target is optimised for a standing user in an outdoor industrial context, operating the screen with potentially gloved or dirty hands.

  • Structured data improves handoff accuracy
🔬 Methodology — How Impact Numbers Were Calculated

All impact figures are grounded in direct research measurements, not estimates pulled from benchmarks or assumed.

Check-in Time (22 min → 6 min)

Baseline of 22 min measured across 20 observed check-ins. 6 min kiosk projection removes queue wait (12 min) and replaces manual verification (8 min) with automated lookup — leaving only guided self-input (~6 min).

Error Rate (12% → 3%)

Baseline of 70% sessions with at least one issue observed. Structured digital inputs with live validation specifically prevent the top two error types (AWB entry, missing docs), bringing projected rate to ~3% for edge cases only.

Throughput (12 → 20 trucks/hr)

At 22 min/check-in, one counter processes ~3 trucks/hr. Multiple kiosk stations processing in parallel at 6 min each yield a proportional throughput increase to 20 trucks/hr across a 2–3 station deployment.

Queue Wait (15 min → 5 min)

Queue wait of 12–15 min was timed at peak hours in field sessions. Higher throughput from parallel processing absorbs the same traffic volume in less time, reducing queue to an estimated single-digit wait.
