The Problem

Fragmented tools create friction at every launch

Preparing content for global product launches requires coordinating product specifications, marketing copy, media assets, and localized content across multiple teams. Before this platform, internal teams relied on a collection of disconnected tools — and the cost was visible at every step.

Problem 01

Complex specification entry

Authors spent excessive time navigating dense, unstructured forms. Required fields were buried, categories had no hierarchy, and there was no way to reuse values from previous products.

Problem 02

Fragmented page assembly

Building a product page meant juggling spreadsheets, shared drives, and a CMS simultaneously. There was no modular canvas — every page was assembled manually from disconnected sources.

Problem 03

Unclear collaboration state

When multiple teams worked on the same entry, there was no shared visibility into who was editing what, what had been reviewed, and which modules were blocked — leading to duplicate work and last-minute conflicts.

Problem 04

Opaque publishing pipeline

The path from "approved" to "live" was invisible. Editors couldn't see where content stood in the publishing pipeline, creating anxiety and miscommunication in the final days before launch.

Operational Context

The real environment where this system lives

Global product launches involve coordinating enormous volumes of marketing content — product specifications, feature copy, imagery, video, and localized variants across dozens of markets — all of which must be ready before a single word goes public.

Internal teams use a secure, access-controlled CMS to prepare, review, and stage all of this content well ahead of announcement day. The platform must support the entire authoring lifecycle while keeping everything strictly confidential until launch.

The content types managed span a wide surface area: structured product data, rich marketing modules, media assets, technical specifications, and localized strings — each requiring different authoring workflows and approval chains.

This means the system must simultaneously support structured content, secure collaboration, and predictable publishing workflows — all without sacrificing the speed teams need under time pressure.

Key Insights from Research

What we learned and how it shaped the design

Observation sessions and stakeholder interviews surfaced four insights that directly informed the system's core design decisions.

1
Launch content involves many stakeholders with different needs.
Design Response
Create collaborative workflows with explicit ownership — so each team can act independently without stepping on each other.
2
Content must follow structured product specifications exactly.
Design Response
Use structured content models instead of free-form editors. Schema-driven entry reduces errors and enables downstream automation.
3
Authors need to see how their content will appear before submitting it for review.
Design Response
Build real-time preview functionality directly into the editor — not as a separate step — to reduce the review-fix-resubmit cycle.
4
Publishing is never a single step — it requires multiple approval gates.
Design Response
Design a transparent, status-based publishing pipeline. Each stage is visible to every stakeholder so no one is left wondering what's blocking launch.
Design Principles

Six principles that guided every decision

Before moving into interaction design, the team established principles to evaluate design choices against — keeping work grounded in the real constraints of enterprise workflows.

Principle 01

Structured Content

Content must follow consistent schemas. Constraints enable consistency at scale and make localization and publishing tractable across dozens of markets.

Principle 02

Collaboration by Default

Multiple teams must work together efficiently. Editing states, reviewer assignments, and ownership should be visible inside the interface — not in email.

Principle 03

Preview Before Publish

Authors must see exactly how content will appear before it enters the review process. Live preview is a core feature, not an afterthought.

Principle 04

Publishing Transparency

Content status must be visible to every stakeholder at every stage — from draft through final approval — so teams always know where they stand.

Principle 05

Scalable Workflows

The system must support complex, multi-team launch processes without requiring manual coordination. Workflow logic should be built into the platform.

Principle 06

Reduce Cognitive Load

Surface only what's needed at each step. Progressive disclosure rather than exposing every field at once — the right information at the right moment.

Design Exploration

Decisions made before the final solution

Early exploration focused on two core problems: how to structure product specifications for efficient entry, and how to define a reusable module system for page building.

Concept A — Rejected

Traditional Form-Based CMS

A flat list of fields for every spec. Too rigid, too dense — marketing teams needed flexibility and product teams needed hierarchy. Discarded early.

+
Concept B — Chosen Approach

Modular Content Editor

Block-based authoring with reusable components. Authors compose from a library of typed blocks. Chosen for its balance of structure and flexibility across user types.

Three-Panel Interaction Model

Tested sidebar-only and inline editing before a three-panel layout emerged as optimal — spec panel, authoring canvas, and live preview all visible simultaneously.

01 — Product Authoring Editor
Structured spec entry alongside a live editing canvas and real-time preview pane.
Problem

Authors needed a single environment to enter product specifications, draft marketing copy, and preview how content would render — without switching between tools.

Design Decision

A three-panel layout: structured spec entry on the left, a modular block-based canvas in the center, and a live preview pane on the right. Spec data links directly into content blocks.

Outcome

Authors can enter specifications and compose marketing content in the same workflow, with real-time visual feedback — eliminating the need for external preview tools.

02 — Page Builder
Drag-and-drop canvas with a pre-built module library and live properties inspector.
Problem

Marketing teams needed full control over page layout and composition, but required developer support to make any structural changes — creating bottlenecks close to launch day.

Design Decision

A drag-and-drop page builder with a library of pre-built, typed modules. Teams compose pages by placing and ordering modules from the sidebar — no code required.

Outcome

Marketing teams can assemble and iterate on complex product pages independently — dramatically reducing round-trips with engineering as launch day approaches.

03 — Asset Manager
Centralized media library with tagging, filtering, and inline usage tracking.
Problem

Launch assets were stored across multiple systems — shared drives, DAMs, and local machines. Authors wasted time hunting for the right file, and outdated assets occasionally made it into final pages.

Design Decision

An integrated asset browser with upload, tagging, and filtering built directly into the platform. Assets are tagged by product, locale, and content type — and usage is tracked across all pages.

Outcome

Authors find the right asset in seconds. Usage tracking prevents outdated assets from appearing in approved content — reducing a significant source of pre-launch errors.

04 — Publishing Workflow
Status-based pipeline with approval gates, scheduling controls, and audit trail.
Problem

Launch content required multiple approval stages before it could go live, but the process was invisible. Teams couldn't see where content stood, who was blocking, or whether the final schedule was still achievable.

Design Decision

A structured pipeline with explicit stages: Editorial Review → Brand Approval → Legal Sign-off → Localization → Scheduled Publish. Every stage has a clear owner, status, and due date.

Outcome

Every stakeholder — not just editors — can see exactly where content stands relative to launch day. Blockers surface early, reducing last-minute scrambles and launch-day errors.

Reflection

What this project taught me

What worked well

Structured content models improved consistency more than any visual decision. The moment we defined schemas, every downstream challenge — localization, preview, publishing — became tractable. Constraints freed us from a thousand small ambiguities.

What was challenging

Designing workflows for multiple teams with conflicting priorities required constant negotiation. The hardest design problems weren't interface problems — they were organizational ones. Making collaboration visible turned out to be as important as any screen.

What I'd improve next

I'd invest in more automation at the handoff points — especially between localization and review. Content validation rules that run inline before submission would eliminate a whole class of late-stage review cycles. Speed comes from reducing correction, not just authoring.
